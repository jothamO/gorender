## The genuinely lightest options

**HTMX + vanilla JS**
Not revolutionary, but it's the right kind of boring. No build step, no bundler, no node_modules. A composition is just an HTML file that reads `?frame=N`. Ships as a single `.html` file. Most developers already understand it.

**Alpine.js**
15kb. No build step. Reactive enough to drive animations off a `frame` variable. Still just HTML files.

**Lit (Web Components)**
~5kb core. Standards-based. A composition is a custom element. Works in any browser forever because it's built on platform primitives. This is probably the most "revolutionary" in the sense of being genuinely forward-looking.

**Pure vanilla JS — no framework**
The actual correct answer for a render contract. Your contract is just:
```js
const frame = parseInt(new URLSearchParams(location.search).get('frame'));
window.__READY__ = true;
```
That's it. Zero dependencies. Anyone can implement it in any language that compiles to JS or runs in a browser.

## The honest take

The "revolutionarily light" framing is a trap. What you actually need for the open source examples is:

1. **A vanilla JS example** — proves the contract has zero lock-in
2. **A React example** — because that's what most people reaching for a Remotion alternative already know
3. **Maybe one more** — Svelte is the strongest candidate here, genuinely lightweight compiled output, no runtime

Svelte is probably your "revolutionary" pick. It compiles away — the output is vanilla JS with no framework runtime. A Svelte composition ships smaller than a React one by a significant margin, and the authoring experience is cleaner for animation-heavy work.

## The split

**For devs** — they want the contract, minimal boilerplate, and to get out of the way. Vanilla JS + Svelte examples. They'll figure it out.

**For non-tech users with projects to render** — they don't want a framework at all. They want to drop their existing thing — a Canva export, an HTML file, a Google Slides-style layout — into gorender and get an MP4 out.

The question for non-tech users isn't "which frontend framework" — it's **"what do they already have?"**

Most non-technical people with "projects to render" have one of:
- A design they made in Figma, Canva, or similar
- A PowerPoint/Google Slides deck they want as video
- A simple webpage or landing page they want animated

None of those are "pick a JS framework" problems.

## What you actually need

No framework decision. No terminal. No contract to understand.

**The key principle in all three templates:** nothing uses `vw`, `vh`, `window.innerWidth`, `Date.now()`, or `requestAnimationFrame`. Everything is driven deterministically from `T = frame / fps`. This is what prevents the preview/render mismatch you were hitting with Remotion.
